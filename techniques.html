technology

sequencing extinct genomes

DNA degrades over time, in part due to a process called cytosine deamination. This process occurs when a cytosine undergoes hydrolysis, and is converted to a uracil. If left unchecked, this creates G to A and C to T mutations in the resulting reads [1]. As such, cytosine deamination is a major inhibitor to obtaining high-quality genomes for extinct species, and processes need to be in place to address its effects.

Paleogenomics comprises a set of techniques for sequencing ancient genomes through the careful extraction of preserved DNA samples. In contrast to modern genome sequencing from live samples, paleogenomic research must take abundant precautions in order to avoid damaging samples. The risk of losing a sample is far greater, as there is only a finite—and usually miniscule—amount of DNA that is preserved well enough to be studied. Samples usually come from fossils or remains that have been well-preserved by their environment. For example, one group of researchers located preserved mammoth bone samples and found a tooth with around 80% mammoth DNA content [2].To then mitigate potential effects of cytosine deamination, the researchers used an enzyme to remove uracils in the DNA. They obtained DNA sequence reads, giving the researchers a complete genome to further analyze. In theory, with more complete genomes, a mammoth reference genome could be created—significantly contributing to mammoth de-extinction efforts.

For more recently extinct species, well-preserved specimens may exist, enabling higher-quality sequencing without additional paleogenomic steps. One example is the Tasmanian Tiger: a recent study seeking to assemble the Tasmanian Tiger's genome used samples from a 108 year old ethanol-preserved specimen [3]. Researchers used genomic analysis programs to quantify cytosine deamination in the Tasmanian tiger genome. However, they found no evidence of deamination, likely due to the relatively recent death of the sample organism, especially in comparison to the mammoth samples in the previous study.

applications of CRISPR

CRISPR-Cas9 is an endonuclease system from bacteria that defends against bacteriophages. However, recent research has revealed that the CRISPR system can be used by researchers to artificially modify DNA in live cells with high accuracy [4]. The implications of this discovery for biological research are immense; for de-extinction work, this means that reintroducing lost alleles to living organisms is a viable way to increase biodiversity. CRISPR would also be a valuable tool in the potential reconstruction of extinct genomes. One company called Colossal is currently working to use CRISPR to stitch together DNA sequences from extinct organisms to construct a full genome (COLOSSAL CITATION HERE). They then plan to create artificial embryos of these species to rebirth extinct lineages.
