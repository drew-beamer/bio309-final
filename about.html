about

If you have seen the Jurassic Park films, you might be concerned to hear that scientists want to bring back extinct species. However, the goal of de-extinction work is not to create a theme park for dinosaurs, but rather to bring back biodiversity through studying recently extinct species' genomes. De-extinction is a subfield of genomics which focuses on reintroducing alleles and/or genomes of extinct species to the biosphere, or, most ambitiously, rebuilding the entire genome with the goal of bringing a species back to life.

While this might sound like scientific fantasy, the field is growing and gaining more traction as a viable counter to the ongoing loss of biodiversity brought about by human activity [1]. Some scientists predict that we are entering a sixth mass extinction event which could threaten life as we know it [2][3]. In order to avert this catastrophe, innovative research such as de-extinction work will become more and more necessary. This may not always look like bringing a Tyrannosaurus rex back to life; more commonly, the goal of de-extinction work will be to reintroduce certain lost alleles into populations, increasing biodiversity more incrementally. However, the field does also include companies seeking to bring species back to life for commercial purposes, indicating that the field has many possible directions for the future.
